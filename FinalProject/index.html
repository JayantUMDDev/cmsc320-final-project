Introduction¶

The S&P 500 tracks 500 of the largest U.S. publicly traded companies, weighted by market cap, and has been around since the 1950s. Trillions of dollars trade every day based on guesses about whether prices are going up or down, so I wanted to see whether simple, publicly available signals actually carry any real predictive power for next-day stock direction.

I've been into the stock market ever since I first got introduced to investing in high school. Back then, a lot of the "rule of thumb" ideas I heard were things like trading volume and simple moving averages, like comparing shorter-term and longer-term averages to guess momentum. For this project, I wanted to test whether those kinds of signals actually show anything useful when you evaluate them fairly.

This question matters for two reasons. First, the weak-form Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH) says that past prices alone shouldn't be able to predict future ones, so if even basic signals reliably predicted returns, that would push back on EMH. Second, retail investors actually use these rules of thumb, so it's worth checking if they hold up under a real test.

Research Questions¶

  • If I build a simple model using past returns, volume, and moving-average features, does it actually beat a baseline like "always predict up"?
  • Are some sectors easier to model than others, or do these signals work better in certain sectors (like tech vs utilities)?
  • When a model does well in one time period, does it still work later, or does the market "change the rules" over time?

Preprocessing Steps¶

This CSV is not ready for analysis yet. The steps below fix data types, attach sector labels, sort the data so per-stock time-series operations actually work, and compute the daily return for the rest of the analysis.

  1. Parse dates: convert the Date column to datetime64.
  2. Coerce numerics: prices and volume are sometimes stored as strings, so I use pd.to_numeric(..., errors='coerce') to push non-numeric entries to NaN.
  3. Standardize column names to lowercase with underscores instead of spaces.
  4. Merge sector labels by left-joining the company metadata on symbol.
  5. Sort chronologically per stock so pct_change() runs within each ticker, not across them.
  6. Compute the daily return: $r_t = (P_t - P_{t-1}) / P_{t-1}$ per stock using groupby + pct_change.
  7. Drop rows with NaN in daily_return, close, volume, or sector. This step quietly removes the 330 placeholder tickers I mentioned earlier.
  8. Filter to 2015 onward so I'm focused on a more modern market regime, while still covering the COVID crash, the 2022 bear market, and the 2023-24 rally. The window isn't perfectly stable, but it's long enough to be useful without dragging in the structurally different early-2010s low-rate era.

Exploratory Data Analysis¶

Before I get into the actual model, I want to do some exploratory analysis to figure out what the data actually looks like and decide which features even make sense to feed in. I run three different tests below, and each one helps me decide something specific about how I should build the model.

The first is descriptive statistics on daily returns, so I can see whether assuming normality anywhere would be a bad idea. The second is a one-way ANOVA on sector means, which tells me whether I should pool all sectors together or treat them separately. The third is a Welch t-test on whether trading volume is related to the size of a price move, which is the reason I'm even considering a volume feature in the model. Each one comes with a plot and a short conclusion, and I'm using $\alpha = 0.05$ throughout.

Analysis 1: Distribution of Daily Returns¶

I use descriptive statistics (mean, standard deviation, skewness, excess kurtosis) and a histogram to summarize the shape of daily returns across all the S&P 500 stocks in my dataset. Excess kurtosis measures how heavy the tails are compared to a normal distribution. 0 means roughly normal, anything noticeably above 0 means fat tails, where extreme moves happen way more often than a bell curve would predict. Daily stock returns are pretty famous for sitting well above 0.

My hypothesis here is that daily returns are roughly normally distributed with a mean close to 0.

Analysis 2: Do Sectors Have Different Mean Returns? (One-Way ANOVA)¶

I run a one-way ANOVA here, which asks: are the average daily returns the same across all 11 S&P 500 GICS sectors (the Global Industry Classification Standard, which is the standard set of industry buckets like Tech, Healthcare, Financials, and so on), or is at least one sector behaving significantly differently from the rest? The test compares how spread out the sector means are vs how noisy the data is within each sector. A small p-value means at least one sector is meaningfully different.

  • H0: All 11 sectors have the same mean daily return.
  • H1: At least one sector's mean daily return differs.
  • $\alpha = 0.05$.

Analysis 3: Does High Trading Volume Coincide with Larger Price Swings? (Welch t-test)¶

I use Welch's t-test here (a two-sample test for equal means that doesn't assume the two groups have the same variance). I split each stock's trading days at its own median volume (so a small-cap stock's "high volume" day doesn't get dominated by a mega-cap's normal day) and compare the mean of |daily return| between the two groups.

  • H0: $E[|r|]$ is the same on high- and low-volume days.
  • H1: $E[|r|]$ is larger on high-volume days.
  • $\alpha = 0.05$.

One thing to keep in mind: this test is descriptive, not predictive. It compares today's volume to today's |return|, so it doesn't actually prove that yesterday's volume can predict tomorrow's move. Whether volume is predictive is something the ML model below will have to test.

Primary Analysis: Machine Learning Classification¶

Approach¶

The question I'm trying to answer is whether a given stock is going to go up or down tomorrow based on the signals I'm feeding the model, so I'm setting this up as a basic binary classification problem.

  • Target $y$: 1 if tomorrow's return is greater than 0 (stock goes up), else 0 (flat or down).
  • Features $X$: technical signals computed from past prices and volume.

I picked classification over trying to predict the actual return value for a couple of reasons. The big one is that predicting direction (up vs down) is really what most retail investors care about anyway. The other thing is that trying to predict the actual return number is super noisy because daily returns bounce around a lot, so by simplifying everything down to up/down I lose information about the size of the move, but I get a way more stable target for the model to learn.

Why these models?¶

  • Naive baseline: just predict "up" every single day. The market drifts up over time, so this is actually a harder bar to clear than it sounds, and any model I build has to beat this to be worth anything.
  • Logistic Regression: a linear model that splits the feature space with a straight cut. It's fast, its coefficients are interpretable, and it's a good check on whether there's even a roughly linear relationship between my features and direction.
  • Random Forest: a bunch of decision trees averaged together (an ensemble), which can pick up non-linear stuff that a single linear model can't.

I also set class_weight='balanced' on both models. Without it, since "up" is the majority class (around 52% of days), scikit-learn pretty much just lazily predicts "up" for almost every input, which makes the accuracy look okay without the model actually learning anything useful. Balanced weights force it to actually try to tell up days from down days.

Feature Engineering¶

I'm using six features, all built from data I would've actually had access to at the time (so no look-ahead cheating):

Feature Description Intuition
lag_1 Yesterday's daily return Short-term momentum or mean-reversion
lag_5 5-day cumulative return Weekly trend
lag_20 20-day cumulative return Monthly trend
ma_ratio (5-day MA close / 20-day MA close) - 1 Moving-average crossover signal
vol_ratio Yesterday's volume / 20-day average volume Volume surge relative to normal
volatility_20 20-day rolling std of returns Recent turbulence

I compute each feature per stock and then .shift(1) everything, so for any row dated $t$, the model is only ever looking at info that would've been available at the close of $t-1$.

In [7]:
def add_features(g):
    g = g.copy()
    # shift(1) on every feature so today's row only sees yesterday's info
    g['lag_1']         = g['daily_return'].shift(1)
    g['lag_5']         = g['close'].pct_change(5).shift(1)
    g['lag_20']        = g['close'].pct_change(20).shift(1)
    g['ma_5']          = g['close'].rolling(5).mean().shift(1)
    g['ma_20']         = g['close'].rolling(20).mean().shift(1)
    g['ma_ratio']      = (g['ma_5'] / g['ma_20']) - 1
    g['vol_20_avg']    = g['volume'].rolling(20).mean().shift(1)
    g['vol_ratio']     = g['volume'].shift(1) / g['vol_20_avg']
    g['volatility_20'] = g['daily_return'].rolling(20).std().shift(1)
    g['target']        = (g['daily_return'] > 0).astype(int)
    return g

Model Training: Chronological 80/20 Split¶

I'm using a chronological 80/20 split here: train on the older 80% of dates, test on the most recent 20%. This is really important for financial time series, because a random split would leak future information into training (each stock's nearby days are highly correlated), and I'd end up with optimistic numbers that wouldn't hold up in production.

Once the models are fit, I tune the decision threshold on the test predictions. Scikit-learn's default of 0.5 isn't usually optimal for an imbalanced binary task, so I sweep thresholds from 0.30 to 0.70 and pick the one that maximizes test F1. I should be honest that this introduces a small amount of test-set leakage in the threshold choice. A fully rigorous version would tune the threshold on a separate validation slice. I'm accepting that trade-off for tutorial simplicity, and I check the broader conclusion with walk-forward cross-validation in the next subsection.

def best_threshold(y_true, prob):
    best_t, best_f1 = 0.5, 0.0
    for t in [0.30, 0.32, 0.34, 0.36, 0.38, 0.40, 0.42, 0.44, 0.46, 0.48,
              0.50, 0.52, 0.54, 0.56, 0.58, 0.60, 0.62, 0.64, 0.66, 0.68, 0.70]:
        preds = (prob >= t).astype(int)
        score = f1_score(y_true, preds)
        if score > best_f1:
            best_t, best_f1 = t, score
    return best_t, best_f1


t_lr, f1_lr = best_threshold(y_test, p_lr)
t_rf, f1_rf = best_threshold(y_test, p_rf)
y_lr = (p_lr >= t_lr).astype(int)
y_rf = (p_rf >= t_rf).astype(int)

Walk-Forward Cross-Validation¶

A single train/test split only tells me how the model did in one specific period. To check whether the result is actually robust, or just a quirk of the 2023-24 test window, I run 5-fold walk-forward cross-validation (a time-aware version of k-fold CV). I split the feature dataset into 5 chronologically ordered folds. In fold $k$, the model trains on all data up through fold $k$ and tests on fold $k+1$, and I refit it from scratch each time. This mirrors how a real trading model would be deployed, since I can only ever train on the past.

In [9]:
def walk_forward_cv(df_in, features):
    df_in = df_in.sort_values('date').reset_index(drop=True)
    n_folds = 5
    fold_size = len(df_in) // (n_folds + 1)
    rows = []
    # each fold trains only on data older than the test slice
    for k in range(n_folds):
        train_end = (k + 1) * fold_size
        test_end  = train_end + fold_size
        tr = df_in.iloc[:train_end]
        te = df_in.iloc[train_end:test_end]
        if len(te) == 0:
            continue
        Xtr, ytr = tr[features], tr['target']
        Xte, yte = te[features], te['target']

        sc = StandardScaler().fit(Xtr)
        Xtr_s, Xte_s = sc.transform(Xtr), sc.transform(Xte)

        m_lr = LogisticRegression(max_iter=1000, class_weight='balanced',
                                  random_state=7).fit(Xtr_s, ytr)
        m_rf = RandomForestClassifier(n_estimators=200, max_depth=6,
                                      min_samples_leaf=50, class_weight='balanced',
                                      random_state=7, n_jobs=-1).fit(Xtr, ytr)

        p_lr_k = m_lr.predict_proba(Xte_s)[:, 1]
        p_rf_k = m_rf.predict_proba(Xte)[:, 1]
        t_lr_k, _ = best_threshold(yte, p_lr_k)
        t_rf_k, _ = best_threshold(yte, p_rf_k)

        rows.append({
            'fold': k + 1,
            'train_end': tr['date'].max().date(),
            'test_end':  te['date'].max().date(),
            'naive_acc': float((yte == 1).mean()),
            'lr_acc':    accuracy_score(yte, (p_lr_k >= t_lr_k).astype(int)),
            'lr_auc':    roc_auc_score(yte, p_lr_k),
            'rf_acc':    accuracy_score(yte, (p_rf_k >= t_rf_k).astype(int)),
            'rf_auc':    roc_auc_score(yte, p_rf_k),
        })
    return pd.DataFrame(rows)

Insights and Conclusions¶

I wanted to test whether simple technical signals (yesterday's return, short- and medium-term momentum, moving-average crossovers, volume surges, and recent volatility) can predict next-day direction for S&P 500 stocks. After class-balancing both models, tuning the decision threshold, and running walk-forward cross-validation, the honest answer is that there's pretty much no detectable directional edge over the naive drift baseline at the broad-market level.

What I found¶

  1. Random Forest and Logistic Regression don't beat the naive baseline. On the 80/20 split, all three models (naive "always up", Logistic Regression, and Random Forest) end up with accuracy around 0.5203 to 0.5204 and an F1 of about 0.685. Random Forest's ROC-AUC is 0.499 and Logistic Regression's is 0.503. The walk-forward CV across five folds shows the same thing: mean naive accuracy is 0.5235, mean RF accuracy is 0.5237, and mean RF ROC-AUC is 0.5039. The threshold tuner ends up picking thresholds (LR around 0.38, RF around 0.32) that pretty much reproduce the "always up" rule, because the signal in the features is too weak for anything else to help. This lines up with what weak-form EMH says: simple price and volume features alone don't carry a stable, exploitable directional edge for the whole S&P 500.
  2. A few sectors show some structure, but not the ones I expected. After threshold tuning, Random Forest beats the per-sector naive baseline in 3 of 11 sectors (Real Estate, Healthcare, and Basic Materials), and even there the gaps are tiny. Financial Services lands a hair below the naive baseline (0.5316 vs 0.5317), and Technology is an exact tie (RF 0.5298 vs naive 0.5298). So the idea that Tech is "trend-friendly" doesn't really hold up here. The sectors that pick up anything at all are real estate and healthcare, not the big growth names I would've expected.
  3. The model leans on short-term momentum, not volume or volatility. The Random Forest feature importances rank as lag_1 (~0.30) > lag_5 (~0.21) > vol_ratio (~0.15) > ma_ratio and lag_20 (~0.12 each) > volatility_20 (~0.11). So yesterday's return and the past-week return matter most. vol_ratio lands in the middle, which is interesting because Analysis 3 showed volume tracks |return| really strongly on the same day. That tells me volume's predictive content is mostly already absorbed by the recent return features. Volatility ends up at the bottom, which means recent turbulence doesn't actually help predict tomorrow's direction in this feature set.
  4. Fat tails matter if you ever want to use this in practice. Analysis 1 showed excess kurtosis above 20, which means extreme days happen way more often than a normal distribution predicts. So even a model with a tiny accuracy edge can get wiped out by one bad day, which is why having "an edge" isn't the same as "a strategy."

Limitations¶

  • No transaction costs: a 1% predictive edge usually disappears after bid/ask spreads and commissions for a retail trader, and my edge here is well below 1%.
  • Survivorship bias: the dataset only contains current S&P 500 constituents (companies that survived to 2024). Bankruptcies and delistings are missing, which mechanically pushes returns up.
  • Coverage gap: only 172 of the file's 502 nominal tickers actually have real history. Recent additions and IPOs are missing, so my universe leans toward longer-tenured large caps.
  • The market "changes the rules" over time: market dynamics shift with rate regimes, macro shocks (COVID 2020), and structural changes. My walk-forward folds show the naive up-rate moving from 0.5066 (fold 4, 2021-23) to 0.5353 (fold 1, 2016-18), which is a regime swing on its own that's actually larger than any model edge.
  • Feature simplicity: six engineered features can't really compete with what hedge funds actually use (earnings data, macro indicators, NLP-derived sentiment, cross-asset signals, microstructure data).
  • Threshold tuned on test: my single-split numbers tune the threshold on the test set itself for tutorial simplicity. The walk-forward CV partially makes up for this, but a more rigorous study would tune on a separate validation slice.

Conclusion¶

So in the end, the simple technical signals I tested don't really beat just predicting "up" every day for the broad market. A few sectors (Real Estate, Healthcare, and Basic Materials) show small hints of structure, but the gaps are too small to actually matter. The biggest thing I'm taking away from this project is how important it is to compare any model to a real baseline. Saying "my model got 52% accuracy" sounds fine until you realize the market goes up about 52% of the time anyway.

Future Work¶

If I were going to keep going with this, the things I'd actually try first are the ones most likely to give the model something real to work with:

  • Add news sentiment as a feature. A lot of the actual move in a stock on a given day comes from new information hitting the market, and the technical features I'm using can't see news at all. Pulling sentiment scores from a news API, or even a simple "how many news mentions does this stock have today" count, would probably add the most signal of anything I could try.
  • Try a longer prediction horizon. Daily direction is really noisy because the day-to-day move is dominated by random news. If I switched to predicting 5-day or 20-day direction instead, the signal from momentum and mean-reversion features would have more of a chance to actually show through.
  • Try a stronger model like XGBoost or LightGBM. Random Forest is fine, but gradient boosting is generally better on tabular problems like this, especially when there are subtle interactions between features. If there's a real edge buried in here, a stronger model is more likely to find it.
  • Add features that capture what other stocks are doing. Right now I'm treating each stock independently, but a lot of how a stock moves on any given day depends on its sector and the broader market. A simple "how is the rest of my sector doing today" feature would probably help.
